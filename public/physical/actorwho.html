Actor Who repurposes Edinburgh's iconic red telephone booths as audition booths for a transmedia performance, letting anyone, anywhere join the Fringe by voting, casting, and auditioning over Twitter. It won a D&AD Yellow Pencil for the BBC brief at New Blood, and later evolved — through testing and a deliberate pivot — into ReCall, a location-based audio memory archive.

The Brief

Why Red Telephone Booths?

"A transmedia experience allowing anyone anywhere to join in on the magic of the Edinburgh Festivals."

The Edinburgh Festivals draw over 50,000 performances and 2.4 million ticket sales a year, but the experience is bound to whoever can physically be in the city. At the same time, Edinburgh's red K6 telephone booths — instantly recognisable, historically loaded, and increasingly obsolete — sit unused across the city center, adoptable from BT for £1 each. That mismatch was the opening: invaluable, iconic real estate doing nothing, next to an audience that couldn't all be there in person.

"There's a feeling of protection inside. It has an elegant-looking design… it's nice and quiet. But mostly it's because they're solid and there's a permanence about them that people like. There's something honest and reliable about them." — T. Hundley, "Support rings out for fading phone booths," 2004
Theory

What Makes a Place, a Place?

"Each aspect, each detail, of this place has a meaning to members of the group, for each portion of its space corresponds to various and different aspects of the structure and life of their society." — M. Boyer, The City of Collective Memory, 2001
"But place and group have each received the imprint of the other. Therefore every phase of the group can be translated into spatial terms, and its residence is the juncture of all these terms." — M. Halbwachs & L. Coser, On Collective Memory

The founding question was less about technology than about memory: when dealing with a location's history, does the collective experience or the individual memory create meaning? And as location history becomes something we carry in our pockets, what happens to a place when its stories can be pulled up anywhere, by anyone, instead of lived on-site?

Research

Digital Archiving & Engagement Marketing

Digital Archiving

The goal of a digital archive is to hold all knowledge, past and present, in one place — and the collective intelligence of that archive surfaces things a single, isolated trail never could.

Engagement Marketing

The ability to psychologically connect with an audience — to move people emotionally and drive action — has always been paramount to advertising. A phone booth offers a way to engage with the city physically, in a way social media and digital content alone can't.

Interactive Design

Concept & Flow

Directors vote on story direction through Twitter polls online; actors audition in person inside the booth. Every episode release triggers a Twitter question, which opens a casting window, which releases the next episode — a loop designed to keep the story alive between physical visits to the booth.

Data Used
  • Downtown phone booth locations
  • Twitter network
  • Audio/video input from the booth's webcam and external mic
Building Actor Who

From Render to Working Booth

The physical build ran in parallel with the software: a full-size phone booth housing a laptop, numeric keypad, and handset, with an Arduino and distance sensor programmed to ring the phone the moment someone walked in.

Testing in the Wild

Exhibition & UX Testing

Five participants ran through a quiz-and-audition script in person; 23 audition videos were collected.

  • More comfortable sharing voice than facial video
  • Wished they could play back their audition and try again
  • "Don't call us, we'll tweet you" leaves you in anticipation
  • Wished there were different play genres available
The Pivot

When the Data Said "Not Quite"

Exhibition testing surfaced a real tension: people loved the booth, the branding, and the physical ritual of picking up the phone — but they were uneasy being filmed without a chance to review it, and wanted more real-time, Edinburgh-specific substance behind the experience.

What Worked

  • Loved the phone booth as an object
  • Liked the physical ritual of the actions
  • Enjoyed the branding and the idea

What Didn't

  • Wanted real-time integration
  • Uneasy about instructions and how to act
  • Nervous about being filmed without review — fine with voice only
  • Some hardware issues

That feedback resolved into four main points: use more data, make the experience more Edinburgh-specific, let people share without feeling self-conscious about how they look, and make it real time.

The new idea: change Actor Who into a geographic, location-based story database in key phone booths around the city — letting people listen to historical tales and add their own space-based stories to a never-ending, real-time timeline.

Prototype 2

ReCall

An audio time machine to call the past, present, and future of a specific location, and allow an individual to add their stories to the space's timeline in real time.

The same booths, the same keypad interaction, but a different purpose: instead of auditioning for a fictional show, visitors listen to real stories tied to that exact spot in Edinburgh, then record and add their own. Grassmarket was identified as the richest starting location, historically.

Data Used

  • Picture libraries
  • Historical databases
  • Audio recordings
  • Geographic data
  • New audio input from visitors

Sources: Edinburgh Collected, Capital Collections, Our Town Stories

Technical Specs

  • Still used the keypad for interface navigation
  • Switched from Processing to HTML, CSS, and Javascript for dynamic, real-time recording uploads
  • Hosted on Heroku for full-stack needs
  • Prototype scope: past and present listening and recording only
V2 Design Iteration

Designing "Recall a Memory"

Just like Actor Who, ReCall went through rounds of UI iteration before the interaction felt right. The first version asked visitors to type a specific year on the keypad — but that meant guessing whether a year existed at all, and more time spent fumbling with the keys. The second version replaced typing with a directional toggle across a fixed timeline, cutting the interaction down to up/down/left/right and confirm.

Five participants tried the second prototype.

  • Liked being able to replay a recording before submitting
  • Satisfying to see a new recording added alongside the other entries
  • Some fumbled with the keypad navigation at first, but everyone "got it"
Looking Forward

Moving Forward

Right now the phone booths only act as a location-based database for voice recordings — but that's a floor, not a ceiling.

  • Pull in Instagram images or tweets taken around Grassmarket automatically
  • Find an interesting way for the booths to "gather" and display that content
  • Swap booths between cities for a day — a Grassmarket booth trades places with one at Piccadilly Circus — and push the location-based element further with AR
